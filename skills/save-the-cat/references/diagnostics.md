# Screenplay Diagnostics

Tools for identifying and fixing common screenplay problems based on Blake Snyder's methodology.

---

## The Hero Leads Test

Your hero must be ACTIVE, not passive. Ask these questions:

### 1. Is the hero's goal clearly stated in the set-up?
- **Yes:** Good. We know what they want.
- **No:** The audience is confused about who to root for.

### 2. Does the hero seek clues, or are clues handed to them?
- **Seeks clues:** Active, engaged hero
- **Handed clues:** Passive protagonist (bad)

### 3. Is the hero active or passive?
- **Active:** Makes decisions, drives the plot
- **Passive:** Reacts, waits, follows

### 4. Does the hero tell others what to do, or ask questions?
- **Heroes never ask questions!** They command. They know.
- Exception: Rhetorical questions or interrogating villains

**If your hero fails this test:** Rewrite scenes so the hero drives action instead of witnessing it.

## Talking the Plot

**The Problem:** Characters say things for the WRITER, not for themselves.

### Symptoms:
- "As you know, Bob..." exposition
- Characters explaining their feelings
- Backstory delivered in monologues
- People saying things they'd never actually say

### The Fix: Show, Don't Tell

**Instead of:**
> "I'm so nervous because my father never believed in me and now I have to prove myself."

**Show:**
- Hands trembling
- Looking at a photo
- A flashback action
- A reaction from another character

### Every Character Has Their Own Agenda

Characters should speak from their own wants, not yours. Ask:
- What does this character want in this scene?
- How are they trying to get it?
- Why would THEY say this line (not why do I need them to)?

## Make the Bad Guy Badder

**The Rule:** Hero and bad guy should be matched in power - with the bad guy having a slight edge.

### Why It Matters:
- Stronger villain = greater heroics
- They're often two halves of the same persona (light and dark)
- The hero must be the underdog

### How to Make the Bad Guy Badder:

1. **Give them more resources**
   - More money, more minions, more weapons

2. **Give them more intelligence**
   - Always one step ahead
   - Predicts the hero's moves

3. **Give them more ruthlessness**
   - Willing to cross lines the hero won't
   - No moral limits

4. **Connect them to the hero**
   - Former mentor
   - Family member
   - Dark mirror

### The Test:
If your hero's victory feels too easy, your bad guy isn't bad enough.

## Turn, Turn, Turn

**The Problem:** Plot moves forward without spinning or intensifying.

### What Should Happen:
- Each scene reveals MORE about characters
- Stakes escalate continuously
- Complications compound
- Speed accelerates toward climax

### Symptoms of Not Turning:
- Scenes feel flat or episodic
- "And then... and then... and then..." pacing
- No surprises or reversals
- Characters remain static

### The Fix:
For each scene, ask:
- What new information is revealed?
- How do stakes increase?
- What flaws, treacheries, or secrets emerge?
- Is this scene faster than the last?

## The Emotional Color Wheel

**The Problem:** Your script only hits one emotional note.

### Full Spectrum Required:
A good movie wrings out the audience emotionally. Include:
- Fear
- Joy
- Sadness
- Anger
- Longing
- Lust
- Humor
- Tension
- Relief

### Even in Comedies:
You need moments of:
- Real danger
- Genuine emotion
- Actual stakes
- Poignant connection

### Even in Dramas:
You need moments of:
- Levity
- Hope
- Connection
- Release

### The Test:
List the emotional beats of your screenplay. If they're all the same color, add variety.

## The "Hi, How Are You? I'm Fine" Test

**The Purpose:** Ensure each character has a unique voice.

### The Test:
1. Cover all character names in your script
2. Read only the dialogue
3. Can you tell who's speaking without the names?

### If Not, Characters Need:
- **Verbal tics** - Catchphrases, speech patterns
- **Vocabulary level** - Educated? Street? Technical?
- **Rhythm** - Short sentences? Long rambling?
- **Agenda** - What they want colors how they speak
- **Background** - Regional dialects? Professional jargon?

### Examples of Distinct Voices:
- Uses profanity constantly
- Speaks in questions
- Never completes a sentence
- Always references sports
- Quotes movies
- Overly formal
- Mumbles asides

## Take a Step Back

**The Problem:** The hero's emotional journey doesn't have enough runway.

### The Principle:
Start the hero as FAR BACK as possible to give them the longest arc.

### Drawing the Bow:
Like an arrow, draw the bow back to its very end for the strongest flight.

### Common Mistake:
Protecting the hero from their full flaw. Making them "too good" at the start.

### Examples of Starting Far Back:
- Scrooge begins as miserable as possible
- Andy in *The Devil Wears Prada* begins as schlubby as possible
- The Grinch begins as hate-filled as possible

### The Test:
Could your hero be MORE flawed, MORE stuck, MORE wrong at the start? If yes, push them back.

## A Limp and an Eyepatch

**The Purpose:** Make minor characters memorable.

### The Technique:
Give every minor character a distinctive visual or verbal hook.

### Examples:
- **Physical:** Limp, eyepatch, scar, unusual height
- **Clothing:** Always wears red, distinctive hat, rumpled suit
- **Behavior:** Nervous tic, always eating, chain smoker
- **Speech:** Accent, catchphrase, stutter

### Why It Works:
- Readers remember characters
- Actors can latch onto something
- Creates visual shorthand
- Enables callbacks

### Running Reminders:
Once established, reference the trait throughout to cement the character.

## Is It Primal?

**The Test:** Are your characters motivated by primal drives?

### The Primal Drives:
- Survival
- Hunger
- Sex
- Protection of loved ones
- Fear of death

### Sophisticated vs. Primal:
**Sophisticated:** "I want to get the promotion."
**Primal:** "If I don't get this promotion, I'll lose my home and my family will starve."

### The Caveman Test:
Would a caveman understand why the hero cares?

- ✓ "Save my children"
- ✓ "Get food"
- ✓ "Find a mate"
- ✓ "Escape the predator"
- ✗ "Increase quarterly earnings"

### Making It Primal:
Connect abstract goals to survival stakes.

## Full Diagnostic Checklist

Run through when your script feels "off":

### Structure
- [ ] Does the Catalyst hit by page 12?
- [ ] Does Break into Two happen by page 25?
- [ ] Does the hero CHOOSE to enter Act Two?
- [ ] Is there a clear Midpoint?
- [ ] Is All Is Lost the opposite of Midpoint?
- [ ] Is there a "whiff of death"?
- [ ] Does the Final Image oppose the Opening Image?

### Character
- [ ] Does the hero pass the "Hero Leads" test?
- [ ] Is the bad guy bad enough?
- [ ] Do all characters have unique voices?
- [ ] Is the hero's journey primal?
- [ ] Does the hero start far enough back?
- [ ] Does every character arc (except bad guys)?

### Scene Craft
- [ ] Does every scene have emotional change (+/-)?
- [ ] Does every scene have clear conflict (><)?
- [ ] Does the plot turn, not just move forward?
- [ ] Are you showing instead of telling?
- [ ] Is the full emotional color wheel used?

### The Basics
- [ ] Is the logline ironic and compelling?
- [ ] Does the title say what it is?
- [ ] Are stakes primal?
- [ ] Do minor characters have "limp and eyepatch"?

## Scene-by-Scene Fix Protocol

For any scene that feels flat:

1. **Identify the +/-**
   - What's the emotional state at the start?
   - What's the emotional state at the end?
   - If they're the same, the scene needs work.

2. **Identify the ><**
   - Who wants what?
   - Who opposes them?
   - Who wins this scene?
   - If there's no conflict, combine with another scene or cut.

3. **Check the Pope in the Pool**
   - Is necessary exposition buried in action?
   - Is there something visually interesting happening?

4. **Apply the Hero Leads test**
   - Is the hero driving this scene?
   - If not, can you rewrite so they are?

5. **Turn up the turn**
   - What new information is revealed?
   - How do stakes escalate?
   - What accelerates?
