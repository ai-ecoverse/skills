# Hero Design ("It's About a Guy Who...")

The hero exists to serve the idea. If the concept is strong but the hero is wrong, the movie breaks.

## Core Rules

- **Hero serves the idea**: Adjust the hero until the premise sparks.
- **Longest journey**: Start them as far back as possible.
- **Primal goals**: Survival, protection, love, hunger, fear of death.
- **Active hero**: They choose, pursue, and drive the plot.
- **Save the Cat**: Give the audience a reason to like them early.

## The "Who" Test

Ask: "Who would this situation be *worst* for?"
That person is usually your hero.

Examples:
- A neat freak forced into a messy world
- A coward forced into heroism
- A cynic forced to love

## The Casting Myth

Write for **archetypes**, not a specific star.  
If only one actor can play it, you've painted yourself into a corner.

## Archetype Guidance

Audiences respond to familiar archetypes:
- Young striver / underdog
- Good girl tempted
- Wounded warrior
- Trickster / jester
- Wise mentor

Use archetypes as **templates**, then customize.

## Hero Tuning Checklist

- Is the hero's flaw directly tied to the theme?
- Does the hero have the longest possible emotional distance to travel?
- Is the hero's goal primal?
- Does the hero choose to enter Act Two?
- Is the hero active, not reactive?
