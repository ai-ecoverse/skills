# The 10 Blake Snyder Genres

These are NOT traditional genres (comedy, drama, horror). These are structural categories based on the underlying story mechanics. Every movie fits one of these ten types.

## Why Genres Matter

Knowing your genre:
- Reveals the rules your story must follow
- Shows what audiences expect (and how to subvert expectations)
- Helps identify what's broken when a script doesn't work
- Provides a library of comparable films to study

---

## 1. Monster in the House

**Examples:** Jaws, Alien, The Exorcist, Fatal Attraction, Panic Room, Tremors, Jurassic Park, A Quiet Place

### The Three Requirements:

1. **A Monster**
   - Supernatural (demon, alien, creature)
   - Human (serial killer, stalker, psychopath)
   - Must be genuinely terrifying and lethal

2. **A House (Confined Space)**
   - Beach town (Jaws)
   - Spaceship (Alien)
   - Family unit (Fatal Attraction)
   - Building (Die Hard - yes, it's also MITH)
   - Must be truly confined - no easy escape

3. **Sin**
   - Usually greed (monetary or carnal)
   - Someone's transgression unleashed the monster
   - The monster is an avenging angel

### The Pattern:
Someone commits a sin → Monster appears → Survivors are trapped → Monster picks them off → Final confrontation with the sin addressed

### Rules:
- The house must be inescapable (if they can just leave, it doesn't work)
- The sin must be identifiable (even if subtle)
- The monster must be unstoppable by conventional means
- "Run and hide" dynamics are central

## 2. Golden Fleece

**Examples:** Star Wars, The Wizard of Oz, Planes Trains and Automobiles, Back to the Future, Road Trip, Ocean's Eleven, Finding Nemo, The Lord of the Rings

### The Elements:

1. **A Road**
   - Physical journey through space
   - Can be literal or metaphorical
   - Each stop represents a milestone

2. **A Team**
   - Often assembled along the way
   - Each member serves a function
   - Often includes a reluctant hero

3. **A Prize**
   - Something tangible being sought
   - Often becomes secondary to internal discoveries
   - The MacGuffin that drives action

### The Secret:
**The theme is always internal growth.** It's not the incidents that matter, but what the hero learns about himself from those incidents.

### Subgenres:
- **Sports Fleece:** Team assembles to win championship
- **Heist Fleece:** Team assembles to steal something
- **Epic Fleece:** Team assembles to save the world
- **Solo Fleece:** One hero, milestones of growth

### Rules:
- Each episode must reveal character, not just advance plot
- The team must bond and change together
- The "fleece" often isn't what it seemed
- Internal journey > external journey

---

## 3. Out of the Bottle

**Examples:** Liar Liar, Bruce Almighty, The Mask, Freaky Friday, Groundhog Day, Blank Check, Big, Click

### Two Types:

**Type A - Wish Fulfillment:**
- Hero is a put-upon Cinderella
- Gets magical wish granted
- Enjoys it, then realizes costs
- Learns to appreciate normal life

**Type B - Comeuppance:**
- Hero is a jerk who needs a lesson
- Gets cursed or forced to change
- Suffers, but finds redemption
- Must have something likeable (Save the Cat moment)

### The Pattern:
Wish/curse granted → Initial enjoyment/suffering → Complications mount → Lesson learned → Return to normal (but changed)

### Rules:
- Magic must have rules and limits
- Hero must eventually choose to give up the magic
- A moral lesson is required
- "It's better to be ordinary" (with the lesson learned)
- The magic can't solve everything - often makes things worse

---

## 4. Dude with a Problem

**Examples:** Die Hard, Titanic, Schindler's List, The Terminator, Breakdown, North by Northwest

### The Two Elements:

1. **A Dude (Ordinary Person)**
   - Just like us
   - Wrong place, wrong time
   - Everyman/everywoman

2. **A Problem (Extraordinary Circumstance)**
   - Life-threatening
   - Unexpected
   - Requires resourcefulness to survive

### The Formula:
The more ORDINARY the dude, the more EXTRAORDINARY the problem can be.

### Key Dynamic:
- The badder the bad guy, the greater the heroics
- Hero triumphs through human qualities: resourcefulness, determination, love
- Often about protecting loved ones

### Common Mistakes:
- Making the hero too capable (becomes Superhero genre)
- Making the threat not threatening enough
- Forgetting the "ordinary" setup

### Rules:
- Hero must be established as normal before the problem hits
- Survival instincts drive the story
- Brain beats brawn
- Stakes are primal: survival, protection of family

---

## 5. Rites of Passage

**Examples:** 10, Ordinary People, Days of Wine and Roses, 28 Days, When a Man Loves a Woman, The Graduate

### The Monster: Life Itself

Unlike Monster in the House, here the monster is:
- Unseen or vague
- Universal
- Inescapable

### Life Stages Covered:
- Puberty
- Adolescence
- Mid-life crisis
- Empty nest
- Addiction
- Aging
- Death of loved one
- Divorce

### The Pattern:
Hero resists change → Life forces change anyway → Suffering and denial → Acceptance → Transformation through surrender

### The Moral:
Always the same: **"That's Life!"**

### Kübler-Ross Parallel:
Often follows stages of grief: Denial → Anger → Bargaining → Depression → Acceptance

### Rules:
- The "monster" sneaks up on the hero
- Everyone else sees it before the hero does
- Victory comes through SURRENDER, not fighting
- Hero must accept forces stronger than themselves
- Pain is the teacher

## 6. Buddy Love

**Examples:** Rain Man, Lethal Weapon, Thelma & Louise, Finding Nemo, E.T., all romantic comedies (Bringing Up Baby, When Harry Met Sally)

### The Secret:
**The best buddy movies work because they follow the emotional arc of a love story — and vice versa. Strip away the romance and a love story is really about two people who need each other.**

### The Three Elements:

1. **Two People Who Hate Each Other**
   - Opposites in key ways
   - Initial conflict
   - Reasons they can't/shouldn't be together

2. **Complementary Opposites**
   - Each has what the other lacks
   - Together they're better
   - Yin and yang

3. **Surrender of Ego**
   - All Is Lost = separation/fight/goodbye
   - Victory requires admitting need
   - One or both must change

### Buddy Types:
- **Equal buddies:** Both change
- **Catalyst buddy:** One buddy enters, changes the other, leaves (E.T., Shane)
- **Pet buddy:** Animal teaches human (Marley & Me)

### Rules:
- Initial hatred must be earned
- The need must be shown, not told
- All Is Lost must involve separation
- Reunion requires character change

## 7. Whydunit

**Examples:** Chinatown, China Syndrome, JFK, The Insider, Mystic River, Citizen Kane, All the President's Men

### Key Distinction:
The "WHO" is far less interesting than the "WHY"

### The Purpose:
To discover something unexpected and dark about human nature - and often about ourselves.

### The Structure:
- Uses a surrogate detective (we follow their investigation)
- Audience pieces together clues with the detective
- The discovery is shocking and disturbing
- Often no clear victory - just horrible truth

### The X-Ray Effect:
These stories turn the x-ray machine back on the audience: "Are WE capable of this evil?"

### Rules:
- Not about the hero changing - about the audience discovering
- Must walk on the dark side
- The "why" must be genuinely surprising
- Often ends ambiguously or darkly
- Truth is more important than justice

## 8. The Fool Triumphant

**Examples:** Being There, Forrest Gump, Dave, The Jerk, Amadeus, Charlie Chaplin films, Mr. Bean

### The Three Elements:

1. **The Fool**
   - Seemingly inept or simple
   - Underestimated by everyone
   - Possesses unlikely wisdom or luck

2. **The Establishment**
   - Institution to be mocked
   - Takes itself too seriously
   - Ripe for skewering

3. **The Insider/Accomplice**
   - Can't believe the Fool is getting away with it
   - Often gets the slapstick punishment
   - Represents the audience's disbelief

### The Pattern:
Fool enters establishment → Everyone discounts them → Fool succeeds through purity/luck → Establishment is humiliated → Fool triumphs or departs unchanged

### Key Setup:
Everyone must discount the Fool's odds for success - repeatedly, in the setup.

### Rules:
- No establishment is too sacred
- The Fool is often unchanged by the end
- Victory through being true to oneself
- The Fool exposes hypocrisy

---

## 9. Institutionalized

**Examples:** One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest, M*A*S*H, The Godfather, Animal House, American Beauty, 9 to 5

### The Core Question:
**"Who's crazier, me or them?"**

### The Elements:

1. **A Group/Institution/Family**
   - Rules and hierarchy
   - Shared goals or purpose
   - Demands conformity

2. **A Naif/Newcomer**
   - Enters the group fresh
   - Asks "How does this work?"
   - Serves as audience proxy

3. **A Breakout Character**
   - Rebels against the group
   - Exposes the group goal as fraud
   - Often sacrificed

### The Tension:
- Loyalty to group vs. personal morality
- Sacrifice of individuality for belonging
- What "family" really means

### Rules:
- Group dynamics must be clearly established
- The institution's rules must seem reasonable initially
- The breakout character pays a price
- Often ends with the naif choosing sides

---

## 10. Superhero

**Examples:** Superman, Batman, Spider-Man, Gladiator, A Beautiful Mind, Dracula, Frankenstein, X-Men

### The Inverse of "Dude with a Problem":
**An EXTRAORDINARY person in an ORDINARY world**

### The Elements:

1. **The Special Power**
   - Extraordinary ability
   - Sets hero apart
   - Blessing and curse

2. **The Nemesis**
   - Equal and opposite
   - Often connected to origin
   - Pushes hero to limits

3. **The Curse**
   - Isolation
   - Misunderstanding
   - Price of being different

### The Creation Myth:
The origin story is crucial. Sequels often fail by forgetting to recreate sympathy for the human side.

### Key Requirement:
**Must stress the PAIN that comes with advantages.** We need to feel sorry for the superhero, not envy them.

### Rules:
- Hero must struggle with their gift
- Power without cost = unsympathetic
- The ordinary world must be shown before the extraordinary
- Human connections ground the fantasy

---

## Genre Mixing

While every movie fits ONE primary genre, elements can overlap:
- Die Hard = Monster in the House + Dude with a Problem
- E.T. = Buddy Love + Superhero
- The Godfather = Institutionalized + Rites of Passage

**Identify your PRIMARY genre first**, then layer in secondary elements.
